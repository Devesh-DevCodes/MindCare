What is Dementia?

Dementia is a condition that affects how the brain works. It can lead to memory loss, confusion, and problems with daily tasks. It is not a normal part of aging and gets worse over time.

Key Facts:

  • Over 55 million people globally have dementia, and 10 million new cases occur every year.
  • Dementia costs the world economy around $1.3 trillion annually.
  • Women are more affected by dementia, both as patients and caregivers.

Symptoms

Dementia symptoms develop slowly and worsen over time. They affect each person differently.

Early symptoms include:

  • Forgetting recent events or conversations.
  • Getting lost, even in familiar places.
  • Difficulty solving problems or making decisions.
  • Misplacing items often.

Later symptoms include:

  • Not recognizing family members.
  • Struggling to eat, move, or communicate.
  • Major personality changes, including aggression.

Causes & Risk Factors

Dementia can result from diseases or brain injuries. Common causes include:

  • Alzheimer's disease: The most common cause.
  • Vascular issues: Reduced blood flow to the brain.
  • Brain injuries: Repeated injuries can lead to conditions like chronic traumatic encephalopathy.
  • Other conditions: Nutritional deficiencies, infections like HIV, or harmful alcohol use.

Risk factors include:

  • Age: More common in people over 65.
  • Lifestyle: Smoking, inactivity, and unhealthy diets increase risks.
  • Chronic illnesses: Diabetes, high blood pressure, and obesity contribute to dementia risk.

Types of Dementia

Different types of dementia include:

  • Alzheimer’s Disease: Affects memory, thinking, and behavior.
  • Vascular Dementia: Caused by reduced blood flow to the brain.
  • Dementia with Lewy Bodies: Linked to abnormal protein deposits in the brain.
  • Frontotemporal Dementia: Affects personality and behavior due to brain degeneration.

Treatment & Care

While there is no cure, treatments and care can improve the quality of life for people with dementia:

Self-care:

  • Stay physically and socially active.
  • Eat a balanced diet and avoid smoking or alcohol.
  • Keep up with hobbies and do things that make you happy.

Support for caregivers:

  • Seek support from family, friends, or professionals.
  • Take breaks to care for your mental health.
  • Learn stress management techniques like mindfulness.

Prevention

While not all cases are preventable, some steps can reduce the risk:

  • Exercise regularly and stay active.
  • Eat a healthy diet rich in fruits, vegetables, and whole grains.
  • Manage health conditions like diabetes or hypertension.
  • Engage in social and mental activities to keep your brain sharp.
